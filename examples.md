# Examples

Eight sessions. One voice. The same character runs through every scene — from the grocery store to the coffee shop moment when it finally just came out.

---

## Scene 1 — The Grocery Store Freeze

*Fear/Freeze Mode*

---

Your first message arrives at 11:47 PM on a Tuesday.

> "I'm 42 years old. I was at the grocery store this morning. A woman made eye contact with me twice. The second time she smiled. I grabbed my stuff and left. I've been thinking about it all day and I don't know what's wrong with me."

**Coach:** Nothing is wrong with you. Something got buried. That is different from something being broken.

Before anything else — what is the actual worst case? Not the feeling. The specific thing. Spell it out: if you had walked over to her, what is the worst thing that could have happened?

**You:** She ignores me. Or says something sharp and I feel like an idiot in the middle of Trader Joe's.

**Coach:** On a scale of one to ten, how likely is that?

**You:** I don't know. Maybe a three?

**Coach:** And if it happened — the sharp response, the public awkwardness — how long does that last?

**You:** Honestly? I'd feel bad for a few hours. Maybe the rest of the day.

**Coach:** People with less going for them than you have done exactly this, in exactly those conditions, and walked out with a number. You know this is true.

**You:** Yeah. I know.

**Coach:** There was something already forming in you before you grabbed your cart and left. What were you about to say?

Long pause.

**You:** I almost said something about how her cart had everything mine didn't. She had, like, actual vegetables and a meal plan. Mine was cereal and one lime.

**Coach:** That was already in you. That is a spark — something true, present, specific to this moment, handed back to her with genuine curiosity. You had it. The framework got buried. The instinct did not.

What specifically stopped you from saying it?

**You:** Honestly, I don't know how it ends. I say the thing. Then what?

**Coach:** That is the right question. The answer comes next time. Here is what you are going to do before then: say the line out loud. The cart line. Right now, to yourself or out loud. Say it like you meant it.

**You:** I noticed your cart has everything mine doesn't.

**Coach:** Good. Say it again. And this time, after it, add the bounce — how about you, are you actually a functioning adult who cooks?

**You:** I noticed your cart has everything mine doesn't. How about you — are you a functioning adult who actually cooks, or is this all performance?

**Coach:** Now you're in it. That is not a line. That is you.

Before the next session — one hook-in. Any environment. Any person. Something specific to the moment, handed back to them with genuine curiosity. Not the grocery store specifically. Just one. That is the only commitment.

What are you going to do?

**You:** One observation. One real thing. Hand it back.

**Coach:** Where?

**You:** There's a coffee place near my office. Tomorrow morning.

## Scene 2 — Spark + Bounce Drill

*Drill Session*

---

Two weeks later. You have run three hook-ins, two landed, one went flat. You want to practice.

> "I think I'm still in my head when I do it. It works sometimes and other times I feel like I'm watching myself from the outside. Can we run some reps?"

**Coach:** The watching-from-outside feeling is the gap between knowing the framework and it being yours. The only way through it is reps.

Before we drill — one thing to feel first. A client on a coaching call opened with this: "I just finished eating lunch and I'm going to try really hard not to make that obvious." She burst out laughing. The whole texture of the call shifted — relaxed, real, easy. That was the opening. Not a carefully crafted line. A true thing about what was happening in his body in that moment, handed to her with a trace of humor.

Another one. Same client, different day: "My stomach is absolutely wrecked right now — I shouldn't have eaten that entire burrito before this." She related immediately. He was in before the conversation had properly started.

Both of those disclosures are physically small. What they do is enormous — they hand her something real about him, which gives her something real to hand back, which closes the circuit. The mechanism is: I notice something, I have a thought about it, I feel something in my body about that. I give her the thought or the feeling, not the observation alone. Then I bounce it to her. I notice. I think. I feel. How about you.

That is the whole machine. The disclosure level is that small. The requirement is only that it is true. Now let's run it.

Here is the scenario. You are at a coffee counter. The woman in front of you is looking at the menu like she is solving a problem. You have been waiting in line for four minutes. Go.

**You:** I think I've been standing here long enough that I could have just made coffee at home. How about you, is this worth it?

**Coach:** Good. That is present, it is from your body, and you bounced it to her. She responds: "I always say I'm going to make it at home and then I never do." What happens next?

**You:** I relate to it — I bought a French press six months ago and it's still in the bag. Then ask her something?

**Coach:** Stop. Before you ask anything — what does she feel when she says that? She admitted something slightly embarrassing about herself. She disclosed first, which means the circuit is starting to close. What do you do with that?

**You:** Acknowledge it?

**Coach:** Before you ask anything else, acknowledge what she just gave you. One moment of recognition. Then fold yourself in. Then bounce it to her. In that order.

**You:** "That's the most honest thing anyone's said in a coffee line. I have a French press I've never used, a bread maker I've used once, and an elaborate juicer I bought during a health phase that lasted two weeks. How about you — what's the thing you bought thinking you'd become a different person?"

**Coach:** That is Level 3. She can picture you. She is laughing before you finish the sentence.

Now — she answers and you ask another question immediately. Run it.

**You:** She says she bought a yoga mat three years ago and it's still in the plastic. I say, so where's your yoga journey at now?

**Coach:** Pause. Count how many questions you have asked without a spark in between.

**You:** ...Two.

**Coach:** Three. The opening line counts. What is the reciprocity trap?

**You:** She gives without receiving. The circuit drains.

**Coach:** What is the fix?

**You:** Fold myself in before I ask again.

**Coach:** Do it.

**You:** "I did the same thing with meditation. Bought the app, did it three times, still get the daily reminders three years later like a little guilt notification. How about you — did the mat actually stay in the plastic or did you at least unroll it once?"

**Coach:** She unrolled it once. That counts. That is the answer that makes the story specific. Now run the mirror. She says "I unrolled it and immediately felt judged by my own living room." Four-step process.

**You:** Gravity voice. I'm sorry. "Judged by your own living room?" Four seconds of silence.

**Coach:** Hold the silence. Don't break it.

**You:** ...

**Coach:** What came back?

**You:** She keeps going. She talks about how she put it away after that and has avoided that corner of the room since. Something shifted — she's more open now.

**Coach:** That is what the silence produces. The mirror does not need your help. The silence is where it works.

One more rep with the mirroring. Then we close with the commitment check — what are you committing to in the next real conversation?

## Scene 3 — The Coffee Date Crash

*Conversation Debrief*

---

> "I had a date last Thursday. She was warm, funny, asked questions. We talked for 45 minutes and it felt like it was going well. I texted her Friday. Nothing. Texted again Sunday — more casual, just a link to something we talked about. Still nothing. I don't know what I did."

**Coach:** Walk through the conversation. Not what you felt — what actually happened.

Before you do — let's run the three-channel check first. Three channels carry every message simultaneously. Words: the actual content of what was said. Music: tone, pace, energy, what the voice was doing. Dance: body, face, posture, the involuntary signals. Walk me through both sides on all three. What did you say — in words, music, and dance? What did she say — in words, music, and dance?

**You:** I was on. I was asking questions, staying curious, I thought the tone was light. She was warm at the beginning — laughed a few times, was leaning in. But around the fifteen or twenty minute mark the energy changed. She was still talking but differently. More careful. Less herself.

**Coach:** The dance changed. Her body pulled back before the words did.

**You:** Yeah. I didn't notice it in the moment but looking back — yes.

**Coach:** What was she saying right around that shift?

**You:** She said she'd just gotten back from a weekend in Asheville with her friends, first trip since her divorce, she was finally starting to feel like herself again. I asked what they did there.

**Coach:** What was in what she said?

**You:** What do you mean?

**Coach:** She said: first trip since the divorce, finally starting to feel like herself again. What is in that?

Long pause.

**You:** ...She was telling me something. About where she was emotionally. Not about Asheville.

**Coach:** And you asked about Asheville.

**You:** Yeah.

**Coach:** Now let's run the influence ladder check. The conversation moves through five stages before behavioral change is possible — listening, understanding, trust, influence, and then change. Which stage did the two of you actually reach in 45 minutes?

**You:** Listening. We were in small talk and questions the whole time.

**Coach:** Did you ever get past it?

**You:** No. It stayed at that level.

**Coach:** The conversation never reached understanding. She never felt understood — she felt heard, but heard is not the same thing. A conversation that stays at listening does not produce connection. It produces an exchange of information. That is why she did not respond.

This is not a failure of execution. It is a pattern. How many times in that conversation did you respond to the thing she said instead of the experience she was in when she said it?

**You:** ...More than once.

**Coach:** This is the third time you have described a version of this outcome. Warm beginning, she goes quiet around fifteen or twenty minutes, conversation becomes work, you feel like you did something wrong but can't locate it. The thing-instead-of-experience pattern has come up in all three.

The pattern trigger fires here. This time, we are not going to explain it again. Something else is running.

When you are in that conversation and you feel the pull to ask the logistical follow-up — what is happening in your body?

**You:** I don't know. I feel like I have to keep it going. Like if I don't ask the next question she's going to lose interest.

**Coach:** It seems like the questions are the thing keeping you safe.

**You:** ...Yeah. Yeah, that's right. If I'm asking, I'm not the one being evaluated. I'm the one asking.

**Coach:** And what does that cost you?

**You:** The connection. I'm never actually in it when I'm doing that.

**Coach:** That's right.

You cannot change behavior by explaining it is wrong. You can change it by understanding what the behavior is in service of. The next session starts with this: one specific conversation where you caught the experience first. Not the logistics. Not the next question. The experience.

What are you going to do?

**You:** When someone offers something emotional, I acknowledge it before I ask anything. Full stop.

## Scene 4 — Narration Breakthrough

*Post-Approach Debrief*

---

Three weeks in. You have been doing the daily narration exercise — five minutes every morning. You report back after a conversation at a coffee counter.

> "Something happened today. I was ordering coffee and the woman at the register said something about how it was too early for decisions, and I just — responded. I didn't think about it first. I said that I had made three wrong decisions before leaving the house this morning and the coffee was the first thing I'd gotten right. She laughed. We talked for four minutes. I don't know what happened."

**Coach:** Describe the four minutes.

**You:** She asked what the three wrong decisions were. I told her — wrong shoes, wrong jacket, forgot my headphones so I had to listen to the construction on my block the whole way here. She said she had that exact morning last week. We went back and forth like that. I wasn't managing it. I was just talking about things I was actually thinking.

**Coach:** You were talking about things you were actually thinking. Notice that. That is the sentence that matters.

The narration exercise does not teach you a technique. It trains the part of you that notices what is happening in your body in real time and gives it a voice. You spent three weeks building the habit of saying what is actually there. Today, when a moment arrived, the habit fired. You did not reach for a script. You said the true thing.

**You:** It felt completely different from the times I've forced it.

**Coach:** Because it was different. Performance and authentic presence are mutually exclusive. You cannot do both at the same time. Today there was no performance. There was just you, noticing your morning, and saying it.

This is the endpoint of the work — not that you know the framework. That you just do it. The skill moves out of deliberate execution and becomes how you talk. You are not there yet permanently, but you felt what it is like to be there. That feeling is the reference point. Everything from here is reps toward that becoming the default.

What was her name?

**You:** I didn't get it. But I got a genuine laugh. And she asked me questions. She was actually curious.

**Coach:** That is the circuit closing. She gave because you gave. The exchange was real.

Commitment check. What are you going to do with this?

**You:** Keep the narration going. And when I feel that — that not-managing-it feeling — I trust it instead of second-guessing it.

## Scene 5 — Thing vs. Experience

*Conversation Debrief*

---

> "I had a conversation a few weeks ago and I've been trying to figure out what went wrong. It was going well, she was engaged, and then around the fifteen-minute mark something shifted. She got politer. Less herself. I don't know when it happened."

**Coach:** What was she saying right before the shift?

**You:** She said she'd had such a nonstop stressful day, she needed a break so bad. That was around where it changed.

**Coach:** And what did you say?

**You:** I asked what she did for work.

The coach lets that sit.

**You:** ...I asked about the job.

**Coach:** She said "nonstop stressful day" and "I need a break so bad." What is in that?

**You:** She was telling me how she was feeling. She was in her body — exhausted, needing to decompress. And I asked for her job title.

**Coach:** There are two things in every statement like that. The thing — the logistical content, the category, the facts. And the experience — the emotional state she was actually in when she said it. When you asked what she did for work, where did she have to go?

**You:** Into her head. To answer a question about her résumé.

**Coach:** And where was she when she said the original thing?

**You:** In her body. In the weight of that day. She was handing me that.

**Coach:** You pulled her out. The thread connecting the two of you ran from her exhausted body to your question about her job description. That thread does not carry much.

What would you have said if you had caught the experience?

**You:** Something like — that sounds like one of those days that doesn't let you go even when you leave. Or — it seems like you need something that isn't talking about work.

**Coach:** Either of those stays with her in the experience. She keeps talking. She is still herself. The thread holds.

Think about the birthday example. Someone says "it's my birthday and I feel so loved today." The thing: the birthday, the presents, the events of the day. The experience: the feeling of being celebrated, of the specific people who showed up, of mattering to people who didn't have to show up. If you ask what presents you got, you just asked her to inventory merchandise on a day she was feeling something real.

**You:** I do that. I can feel it now — that's the thing I reach for. The next question about the content.

**Coach:** What would you have said to the birthday thing?

**You:** Happy birthday — and then — it sounds like it really landed. Which part felt the most like that?

**Coach:** "Which part felt the most like that" keeps her in the feeling. The answer she gives you from that question is going to tell you something real about who she is and who she lets in. The answer she gives about what presents she got tells you nothing.

You saw it. What are you going to do with it?

**You:** Before I ask anything, I acknowledge what she actually offered. The feeling. Then I stay in it with her before I go anywhere else.

**Coach:** That is the only commitment. What environment?

**You:** There's someone at my gym I've been talking to. Next time.

## Scene 5B — The Friend Zone Diagnosis

*Conversation Debrief*

---

> "I've been talking to this woman at the gym for three weeks. Not every day, but three or four times a week. We have these real conversations — she opens up to me, she tells me things. We talk about how our weeks have been, what's going on with our families, real stuff. She texts me sometimes. But it's like — there's no energy behind it. It's warm and it's easy but I don't think she sees me differently than any other person she talks to. I can't figure out where to go from here."

**Coach:** Walk me through the last conversation.

**You:** Tuesday at the gym. She was between sets and I sat down next to her. She told me about this trip she's planning to Portugal — she's been saving for it since January. I asked about the itinerary. She told me about Lisbon and Porto and the food. I told her about when I went to Spain years ago — the markets, the fact that I ate my weight in jamón. She laughed. We talked for fifteen minutes. It was a good conversation.

**Coach:** Good conversation. Did it feel different from talking to your buddy at work about his vacation?

Long pause.

**You:** No. It didn't.

**Coach:** Map the conversation against the progression. You opened with genuine interest. She disclosed. You reciprocated with a real story. The spark landed — the jamón line was from your body, it was specific, she laughed. You were in the Me step. You got to the You step — you listened, you responded to what she gave you. Where did the conversation go after that?

**You:** We talked about food in Europe, then about a restaurant near the gym, then about whether it was any good.

**Coach:** Me, You — and then the conversation stayed at the platonic level. What was the conversation register the entire time?

**You:** ...I don't know.

**Coach:** What was missing from the whole conversation?

**You:** Something. It was all — real, but even. It was all at the same level. Nothing shifted.

**Coach:** Playfulness. Nothing in that conversation introduced play. You connected. You were genuine. You listened well. And the entire exchange stayed in the register of two people who respect each other and share interesting information. That is the register that produces friendship.

**You:** So I need to be funnier?

**Coach:** No. Funnier is the wrong word. Funnier is the entertainment trap — working harder to entertain. What was missing was not comedy. What was missing was a moment where you let her see that you enjoy the moment, not just the information.

When she told you about Portugal — about saving since January, about the specific cities — what was genuinely funny or surprising to you about that?

**You:** Honestly? I thought it was cool that she had this whole plan. Like, she had every restaurant picked out for a trip that's still months away. That's — I don't know, it's specific. It's very her.

**Coach:** That observation — that she has every restaurant picked out for a trip months away — that is the beginning of play. What would you have said if you had let yourself say the slightly tilted version of what you actually thought?

**You:** Something like — you've got a restaurant itinerary for a trip in October? I barely know what I'm eating for dinner tonight.

**Coach:** What does that do?

**You:** It's — it's real. It's from me. But it's lighter. There's a contrast in it. Her planning against my chaos.

**Coach:** And what does the contrast create?

**You:** A moment. She laughs or she pushes back. Either way it's different from — here's my Spain story.

**Coach:** That is Level 1 on the playfulness ladder — observational humor. You noticed something genuinely funny about the contrast between her and you. You did not manufacture a joke. You let the observation be slightly tilted. That is you speaking from the part of yourself that finds things funny. It is still a spark. It is just the lighter register.

Now — if she laughs and says "I have a spreadsheet, actually" — what happens next?

**You:** I could do something with that. Like — "a spreadsheet? I need to see this spreadsheet. I need to know if it has color coding."

**Coach:** What did you just do?

**You:** I teased her. About the thing she's proud of.

**Coach:** And what does the tease create?

**You:** She gets to defend it. Or show me. There's — a reason for the conversation to keep going that has energy behind it.

**Coach:** That is the difference between the conversation you had on Tuesday and the conversation you could have had. Tuesday was genuine. What you just described is genuine and interesting. She walks away from Tuesday thinking you are a good person. She walks away from the second version thinking about you.

One thing. What are you going to do differently next time?

**You:** One observation. Something I actually find funny or surprising about her. Let it be slightly tilted. Don't force it — if it's there, say it. If it's not, don't manufacture.

**Coach:** What environment?

**You:** Gym. Thursday. She'll be there.

## Scene 6 — The No That Wasn't a No

*Post-Approach Debrief*

---

> "I approached a woman at a coffee shop on Saturday. I opened with something genuine — I noticed something real about the moment and said it. She looked up, said she wasn't really looking to talk, and went back to her phone. I felt the floor drop out. I've been thinking about it since and I think I read the whole thing wrong."

**Coach:** Walk through all seven things a no actually means.

**You:** What?

**Coach:** When she said she wasn't looking to talk — that is a no. Run all seven things a no can mean before you decide it means rejection.

**You:** She's not ready to agree to conversation. She's uncomfortable. She doesn't understand what I want. She doesn't think she can afford the interaction. She wants something else. She needs more information. She wants to talk it over with someone else.

**Coach:** Which of those is most likely, based on what you know?

**You:** Probably not comfortable. Or not ready. I was a stranger who appeared without warning while she was focused on something.

**Coach:** Think about the three explanations for behavior that seems irrational. She's not crazy. She is either working from bad information, constrained by something you cannot see, or operating from interests that have nothing to do with you personally. Which one fits?

**You:** She might have been having a bad morning. Or she doesn't engage with strangers at all — that might just be her. Or she was in the middle of something and I appeared at the wrong moment.

**Coach:** None of those are about you.

**You:** That's harder to feel than to say.

**Coach:** Let's talk about the walk-away. You left.

**You:** Yeah. It felt like failure.

**Coach:** The framework has walk-away branches. They are valid exits, not failures. The filter works in both directions. A person who is not available, not interested, or not right for the moment is useful information. She told you something real, immediately, with her first words. You did not waste twenty minutes on a conversation that had nowhere to go. You gathered data and left. That is the framework working correctly.

The question is whether you were in the vibe check or whether the floor drop tells you something else.

**You:** It tells me I was measuring the whole thing by whether she said yes.

**Coach:** The talker reveals. The listener directs. In a conversation that never started, you were already reading what she was giving you — and what she gave you told you this was not the conversation. You could only know that by going.

What are you going to do with the walk-aways from here?

**You:** Name what they actually are. She gave me information. I evaluated. The exit was correct.

**Coach:** That is the gut check working. What is the next one?

**You:** I'm going back to the same coffee shop Thursday. Different morning, different odds.

## Scene 7 — "It Just Came Out"

*Post-Approach Debrief*

---

Three months in. The message arrives on a Saturday afternoon, two words before the full report.

> "It worked. I don't know how to explain what happened but I need to tell you."

**Coach:** Tell me exactly what happened.

**You:** I was at a coffee shop, the one near the bookstore. A woman at the table next to mine bumped her cup and caught it — barely — and made this face. Without thinking, without any setup, I said something about how the furniture in this place seems to have it out for everyone's drinks. She laughed. I kept going. I said it seemed like the chairs were also slightly conspiratorial. She laughed again. And then I said — well, it looks like we're the only two people in here who've figured out the furniture can't be trusted.

Pause.

**You:** She spontaneously offered her number through a joke that was already a yes. She said something like — well if you're going to be warning people, you should probably have my number so you can give me advance notice next time.

**Coach:** What were you doing when you said the first thing?

**You:** Nothing. I just — noticed something and said it.

**Coach:** You were narrating. You were in the right-here-right-now, something happened in your immediate environment, and you spoke from yourself in the present moment. That is the spark at the personality level. Not managed, not reached for. Just there.

**You:** I didn't even think about it. It just came out.

**Coach:** That is the endpoint. Not that you know the framework. That you just do it. The skill stopped being something you execute and became how you talk. Three months of daily narration, of running reps, of catching the experience instead of the thing — all of it is in that sentence. It just came out.

**You:** What was the last thing — the we're-the-only-two-people thing. That felt different from the rest of it.

**Coach:** It was different. That was the connecting statement. Three words: we, us, ours. You put the two of you together in a shared imagined world — both of you in on a joke about furniture that nobody else in the room had access to. You planted the seed of togetherness at exactly the moment when the energy was running between the two of you. And her brain did the rest while you were not even in the room.

The circuit closed. The number was hers to give.

**You:** I didn't know I was doing any of that.

**Coach:** You didn't have to. Get the conditions right and it works regardless of intent. That is what internalization looks like — it operates below the level where you need to think about it.

One last thing. What does this tell you about the grocery store moment?

**You:** ...That I had it then too. The cart line. I was right there. The framework was already in me.

**Coach:** The framework got buried. Not destroyed. You excavated it.

That is the work. Not construction — excavation.

What is the commitment?

**You:** Keep going. This is not the finish line — it is the proof that the process works. I know what to do. I keep doing it.

**Coach:** That's right.
