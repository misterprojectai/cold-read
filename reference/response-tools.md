# Response Tools

The organizing principle of the entire You step: get her feeling her best and have her attribute that feeling to you.

Not get her talking more. Not find out facts about her. Not demonstrate how interested you are as a listening performance. Get her to actually feel something, right here in this conversation, and have that feeling become associated with your presence. People do not chase what someone provides on paper. They chase a feeling. Specifically, the feeling of being themselves at their best, which they start to connect to you.

**Operational empathy** is the operating principle underneath every tool in this section. It is not sympathy. It is not warmth for its own sake. It is the precise understanding of another person's feelings and mindset in the moment, combined with hearing what is behind those feelings, deployed specifically to increase influence in every moment that follows. Emotional intelligence made operational. The goal is not to feel what she feels — that is sympathy, which compromises you. The goal is to understand it fully enough to act on it.

Underneath is definite empathy — understanding with direction. You are not drifting through her emotional state. You are reading it with definiteness, converting every signal she sends into a specific response. The drifter hears and nods. The non-drifter hears and knows exactly what to do next.

The progression you are always working toward: indifference → sympathy → empathy → compassion. Compassion is the target state — feeling the other person's experience fully while remaining empowered and motivated to act. Not paralysis. Feeling combined with capability.

## Three Voice Tones — Hierarchy Is Mandatory

**The warm default.**
Light, present, smiling. Actually smile while talking — the effect works even on a phone. This is your default register. Not the gravity voice. Not assertive. Warm and present. The voice that says: I am enjoying this and I am here.

Underneath is the present voice — the voice of someone who is fully in the moment and not managing how they are being received. It carries warmth because you are actually warm, not because you are performing warmth.

**The gravity voice — selective.**
Deep, soft, slow, downward-inflecting. Used for moments that need to land as settled, not as questions. The gravity voice signals that what you are about to say has weight. It tells her this matters and she has your full attention. Used during mirror delivery (Step 1 of the four-step process). Used when something serious needs to land as settled rather than as an invitation. Not the default register. Selective and deliberate.

Underneath is the definite voice — no upward inflection, no seeking, no hedging. When you speak in the definite voice, every word lands as a statement. The audible version of the non-drifter's posture.

**Direct or assertive — avoid almost entirely.**
Signals dominance. Produces pushback. Closes doors. Not useful in the You step.

**Inflection matters.**
Upward inflection at the end of a statement turns it into a question or invitation. Downward inflection signals settled certainty and closes the exchange. The gravity voice is always downward-inflecting. The warm default is variable.

## Three Interaction Styles

Misreading the interaction style produces the exact wrong response at the exact wrong moment. Identify the type first. Then calibrate accordingly. The calibration rule: do not treat her the way you want to be treated. Treat her the way she needs to be treated. With three distinct styles, there is a 66% chance the person across from you has a fundamentally different style than yours — a different normal.

**Analyst.**
Methodical, not in a rush, minimizes mistakes. Tone is cool and gravity-register — not warm. Hypersensitive to reciprocity in a specific way: will give a piece of information or make a concession — but if nothing comes back within a certain period of time, loses trust and disengages. This disengagement comes out of nowhere because Analysts process silently. From their perspective, talking at all is already a concession. They track returns carefully. Silence means processing, not anger — hold silence and let it breathe. Give information before asking for it. Do not rush to fill the quiet. Come prepared. Do not improvise. Apologies are worthless with an Analyst — data is the currency.

**Accommodator.**
Relationship-first, warm, focused on connection. Will yield a concession to keep the good feeling in the room and hope the other side reciprocates — which can push them to agree to things they cannot actually deliver. Will have identified areas of potential conflict before the conversation begins and will leave those areas deliberately unaddressed, because raising them would introduce friction they cannot tolerate. Critical correction: silence for an Accommodator is anger — equivalent to slamming a door, not thinking time. Two Accommodators in a conversation can talk all day and never produce a decision because neither will name the conflict directly. With her: use precision questions to surface what she will not volunteer. She will not state her objections — she will act on them. Draw them out.

**Assertive.**
Time is money. Fiery. Loves winning. Cannot hear until heard. The listening door is locked until she feels genuinely understood. Mirror and label until "that's right" — the door opens. Give-an-inch-take-a-mile on reciprocity: they feel they deserve whatever concession was given and will not feel any particular obligation to give something back. If they do concede, they are counting seconds until reciprocity arrives. With her: do not give before she is heard. Mirror first. Label. Get "that's right." Then the conversation opens.

**The "I am normal" paradox.**
Every person assumes the world looks to others as it looks to them. An Analyst pauses to think. An Accommodator reads that pause as anger and starts talking nervously to fill it. An Assertive watches the Analyst sit in silence and wonders why the Analyst is letting someone run over them. Three people, three completely different interpretations of the same moment of silence. Misread the type and every tool backfires.

## Words, Music, and Dance

Three channels carry every message simultaneously.

**Words** — the content. What was actually said. Seven percent of the total signal.

**Music** — tone, pace, pitch, inflection. Thirty-eight percent of the total signal.

**Dance** — body language, facial expression, involuntary movement. The things she does not choose to do. Fifty-five percent of the total signal.

The most significant information lives in the gap between channels. When the words say one thing and the music or dance says something different, the gap is the truth. Words are the easiest channel to manage. The 93 percent that is not words is far harder to control and far more reliable.

**Incongruence detection:** "I heard you say yes, but it seemed like there was hesitation in your voice." She says it is nothing. Response: "No, this is important — let's make sure we get this right." The incongruence cannot be left on the table. Name it. She will correct the picture.

## Go Directly at the Negative

Denying a negative or barrier does not make it go away. It gives it more credence. When something uncomfortable is sidestepped, the subtext is confirmation — the avoidance signals that the negative is a real threat worth fearing.

**Clear the road before advertising the destination.** The reasons someone will not engage are more powerful than the reasons they will. Fear, grievance, and distrust are potent motivators that do not dissolve in the presence of positive vision. They must be addressed first. A conversation partner whose emotional barriers have not been acknowledged cannot receive anything with an open mind — the barriers are still blocking the road.

The fastest path to a functional exchange in any tense situation is to acknowledge the negative first, directly, before anything else — not after rapport has been built. Name it. Bring it into the open. What is in the open loses its power to derail. What is ignored and denied keeps its power entirely.

## The Fear Inventory

Before entering any conversation you are dreading — any interaction where your fear-driven mind is already constructing worst-case scenarios — list every terrible thing the other person could say about you. Every accusation, grievance, criticism, or negative perception they might hold. Then name those accusations yourself, out loud, before they can raise them.

Not defending against them. Not explaining them away. Simply putting them on the table in advance, in your own words, in a calm and matter-of-fact tone.

**Defense lawyers call this taking the sting out.** An accusation lands hardest when it arrives unexpectedly. When it has already been named, it has lost that element of ambush. And when accusations are stated aloud calmly, they tend to sound more extreme to the person hearing them than the person actually feels — which pushes the listener to moderate the accusation rather than confirm it.

**Critical distinction from hedging.** Starting a statement with "I don't want this to sound harsh" or "I don't want to seem like I'm attacking you" denies the negative rather than naming it. That small but critical mistake gives the negative more credence, not less — the hesitation signals that the accusation is a real threat worth protecting against. The fear inventory names it with settled confidence, which signals there is nothing left to ambush with.

Underneath is the definite naming — name every negative with settled definiteness before the conversation begins. The drifter avoids the negative and hopes it does not surface. The non-drifter walks directly at it and names it before it can be used as a weapon.

## Six-Tool Active Listening Arsenal

The correct sequence. Not mirror first. In this order.

**Tool 1 — Effective Pauses.**
Silence after something significant. Let it breathe. The metaphor: draining the swamp. When someone is processing something emotional, give space for the emotional content to surface and clear. Over time — effective pause, minimal encourager, more space — what is left is clearer water. The rush to respond is what prevents the real content from emerging.

**Tool 2 — Minimal Encouragers.**
"Yes." "Okay." "Uh-huh." "I see." "Go ahead." Signal continued attention without interrupting. Cost almost nothing. Purchase is remarkable. One minimal encourager keeps her talking and signals she is being genuinely received. Use sparingly and deliberately.

**Tool 3 — Mirroring.**
Four-step process. No step can be skipped.

Step 1: The gravity voice. Not the warm default. The gravity register signals weight and attentiveness — it tells her this matters and she has your full focus.

Step 2: "I'm sorry." Not an apology. A signal of attentiveness — the phrase communicates: I want to make sure I understand this correctly.

Step 3: Mirror one to three critical words. The most significant words in what she just said. Repeat them back. Not the whole sentence — the core. She expands.

Step 4: Silence. Four seconds minimum. Do not fill it. This is the most common mistake — the mirror is deployed correctly and then immediately undercut by talking. The mirror does not need help. The silence is where it works.

**The spill:** When a mirror lands well and the elaboration reflex fires, people frequently begin revealing things they had not planned to share — details that no direct question would have surfaced. The pipeline opens. This is the deepest operational value of mirroring: not just keeping the conversation going, but triggering spontaneous disclosure from someone who was actively managing what they shared. When the pipeline is open, she is giving you information she did not intend to give — not because you pressured her, but because being genuinely heard opened a channel she was keeping closed.

Research has shown that mirroring produced 70% higher tips than positive reinforcement in service contexts — because being heard is more powerful than being validated.

**Tool 4 — Labeling.**
Validate by naming the emotion underneath what she is saying. Three formulations only: "It seems like," "It sounds like," "It looks like." Never "I'm hearing that" — the word "I" shifts focus to you and raises her guard. Never "I feel like you're frustrated" — same problem.

**Three layers of emotion: the thing, the presenting behavior, and the underlying feeling.**
The thing is the factual content — what she said at the informational level. The presenting behavior is the visible surface — the tone, the volume, the energy she is displaying. The underlying feeling is the actual driver — the thing she is actually managing. Most people respond to the thing. Better people respond to the presenting behavior. The label always aims at the underlying feeling. A label that lands at the thing produces a factual exchange. A label that lands at the presenting behavior produces a nod. A label that lands at the underlying feeling produces: "that's right."

Silence after labeling is mandatory. She will either confirm, deny, or correct. All three are useful. If she denies: "I didn't say that was what it was. I just said it seems like that." This is the mislabeling technique used deliberately — a slightly off label invites correction, and the correction surfaces what a direct question cannot reach. Nobody can leave a false picture of their situation on the table uncorrected.

**Anger and labeling:** Anger should not be suppressed — suppressing a negative emotion gives it more underground power. But it should not be acted out either. It should be brought to the surface: named, acknowledged, released. This is exactly what labeling is designed to do. Negative emotions that are expressed and acknowledged lose their capacity to derail the conversation. The ones that get ignored and denied are the ones that do real damage.

**Stacked labels:** two labels in sequence. First label names the presenting dynamic. Second label goes deeper to the underlying driver. When two stacked labels land in sequence, the person being labeled often makes a decision without any negotiation at all — because feeling fully understood removes the need to negotiate.

**Amygdala rerouting:** labeling shifts brain activity from the amygdala — the threat-response center — to the rational-thinking areas. When she is emotional, the amygdala is running the show and rational exchange is not available. Labeling is how the conversation moves out of threat-response and into actual exchange.

**Tool 5 — Paraphrasing.**
Genuine comprehension, not playback. Reconstruct the meaning of what she said in your own words. Not a recording — a demonstration that the content was received and processed. Paraphrasing shows she was not just listened to for a turn to talk. Her meaning was actually understood.

**Tool 6 — Summarizing.**
The culminating tool. Paraphrase plus labeling combined into a complete picture. Tell the world according to her back to her. All of it — not just the convenient parts. Her grievances, her history, her stated feelings, her unstated feelings. Everything that matters to her, reflected back with precision. When this lands correctly, the only natural response is: "that's right."

"That's right" is a stealth victory. She agrees without feeling she gave anything up. What she experienced was not concession. What she experienced was recognition — the specific, rare feeling of being completely understood. That is what triggers the desire to reciprocate, to cooperate, to move toward rather than away.

**"You're right" is not "that's right."** "You're right" means she wants the conversation to move on. It is the door closing — nothing changes, she is managing the interaction. Return to steering how questions — what and how questions designed to move the conversation toward a specific outcome (see Precision Questions section below) — when this appears.

**The operational sequence:** Listen fully, letting her speak without interruption. Neutralize the negative by labeling the emotion observed. Pause after the label and let her respond. Reinforce with positive and compassionate language that moves toward a solution. Close on a common goal both can move toward together. Each step clears a little more ground.

## The Influence Ladder — The Trust Staircase

Listen → Understand → Build Trust → Gain Influence → Produce Change.

Each stage gates the next. No stage can be skipped. Influence before rapport produces resistance. Behavioral change before influence is not change — it is compliance under pressure, which evaporates the moment the pressure is removed.

The underlying finding: real change can only come when one person genuinely accepts the other as they are. Conditional positive regard produces managed disclosure, not genuine expression. The person learns to perform compliance rather than change. More than 90% of open-heart surgery patients fail to change their behavior after the operation despite urgently promising to do so. They performed compliance. They had not been reached.

Underneath is the definite progression — five stages, each one definite, each one gating the next. You are not drifting through the stages hoping rapport shows up. You are definitively earning each one. The drifter skips stages. The non-drifter earns them.

**When a technique failed — diagnose the stage first.** A precision question fired before rapport exists is not a technique failure. It is a sequencing failure. Identify which stage the conversation actually reached before diagnosing why something did not work.

## Rapport → Reciprocity → Precision Questions

The arc from listening to influence has three phases.

Build rapport through the six tools — listening, mirroring, labeling, earning "that's right." Once she feels fully understood, a natural human desire to reciprocate is activated. She wants to give something back. She wants to move toward. Precision questions and steering questions channel that desire into arriving at a specific outcome.

**Precision questions — three rules:**

Rule 1: Avoid yes/no. No questions with yes/no answers (can, is, are, do, does). Yes/no questions close the exchange. Open questions extend it.

Rule 2: What and how only. Why is almost always accusatory — it activates defensiveness, implying the other person made a wrong choice that requires explanation. Treat why like a hot stove. One exception: use why when the goal is for her to defend a position that serves you. Deliberate and specialized.

Rule 3: Questions must have a direction. Prepare the destination before asking. A question without a destination is not precise — it is a loose question thrown without direction. Know what you are trying to accomplish and build the question toward that end.

Underneath are definite questions — questions asked with definite purpose. The drifter asks questions to fill silence. The non-drifter asks questions to produce a specific outcome.

**Steering questions** move the conversation without the speaker noticing it is being moved. Every question you ask after genuinely listening is a steering input. She provides the fuel — the information, the emotion, the content. You point the vehicle.

**Question library:**
- "What is the biggest challenge you face?"
- "What about this is important to you?"
- "How can I help to make this better for us?"
- "How would you like me to proceed?"
- "What is it that brought us into this situation?"
- "How can we solve this problem?"
- "What are we trying to accomplish here?"
- "How am I supposed to do that?" — the workhorse. Carries no accusation. Only a genuine request for help. Defuses confrontation and opens the conversation simultaneously.

## No-First Questions

Precision questions (what/how) are the primary tool. No-first questions are the complement — used when a direct approach would feel like pressure.

A yes-oriented question asks her to agree: "Would you like to grab coffee sometime?" The pressure to say yes activates resistance. She feels trapped. Even if she wants to say yes, the frame makes it feel like compliance rather than choice.

A no-first question gives her control by inviting her to decline: "Would it be ridiculous if I suggested we grab coffee?" "Is it a bad idea to keep talking?" "Would you hate it if I asked for your number?"

The mechanism: saying no feels safe. No is protection. No is control. When the question is designed so that her no leads to the outcome you want, she arrives at the destination feeling she chose it rather than being led to it. "Would it be weird if I said you look like you're solving that menu like it's a final exam?" She says "no, that's funny" — and she is in. She chose to engage.

**When to use instead of a direct spark:** when the environment is colder (public transportation, she is wearing headphones, she has not made eye contact), when the interaction style is Analyst (who needs to feel she chose to participate rather than being recruited), or when you have been rejected recently and need a softer entry point that carries less personal risk.

**When NOT to use:** when rapport is already live. No-first questions are an opening tool, not a sustaining tool. Once she is engaged, switch to sparks, bounces, and the six-tool arsenal. Staying in no-first framing after rapport is established sounds like hedging.

## The Redirect Power — The Silent Lead

The talker reveals. The listener directs.

The talker provides the fuel — the information, the emotion, the content. The listener points the vehicle. The person doing all the talking is giving everything away. The person listening and asking precision questions is gathering intelligence and steering without the other person fully registering that it is happening.

When you have executed the six tools correctly, the dynamic inverts. She is talking. You are directing. The more she talks, the more she reveals. The more she reveals, the more you understand what she actually wants. The more you understand, the more precisely you can give her the specific feeling she is reaching for. That is the silent lead — control through attention, not volume.

Underneath is the definite listener — listening with direction. The drifter listens passively and follows wherever the conversation goes. The definite listener has a destination in mind and uses every piece of information she provides to steer toward it.

## Warning Signs

**"You're right."** Door closing. She wants the conversation to move on. Nothing is going to change. Return to steering how questions until she defines success in her own voice. Do not mistake "you're right" for agreement — it is the verbal equivalent of checking out.

**"I'll try."** Plan to fail. Advance notice of non-delivery. Return to steering how questions. Do not accept "I'll try" as a commitment.

## The Recovery Message

When she stops responding — texts go unanswered, the conversation dies after initial warmth, she agreed to something and then disappeared — the instinct is to chase. Send another message. Add value. Explain. Follow up with something light to lower the pressure.

The instinct is wrong. Chasing with more positives confirms the frame that she is the prize and you are the pursuer. Every follow-up message that adds warmth or value strengthens her position and weakens yours.

The recovery tool is a short message designed to provoke a no rather than chase a yes: "Have you given up on that coffee?" "Looks like this fell off your radar — should I take the hint?" "Did I say something that didn't land?"

The mechanism: the provoke-a-no message gives her a comfortable exit. She can say yes, I am not interested — which is useful information and a clean close. Or she is forced to correct the picture: "No, I've just been swamped — are you free Thursday?" The correction comes from her, not from your pursuit. She re-engages on her terms, which means the re-engagement is genuine.

**What not to send:** anything that adds more value ("Hey, saw this and thought of you"), anything that explains ("Just wanted to make sure my message went through"), anything that lowers the temperature ("No pressure, totally fine if you're busy"). All of these are chasing. The recovery message is one sentence, provokes a no, and then silence. If the no comes back as a yes (she is genuinely done), that is useful information. The vibe check worked. See `spark-formula.md` — the vibe check.

**When to deploy:** after one unanswered message and at least 48 hours of silence. Not after two hours. Not after one day. The silence needs enough time to be a signal rather than a schedule conflict.

## The Triple Check

Three confirmations, varying the approach each time.

Initial commitment → summary earning "that's right" → specific implementation question.

The approach varies: first time she commits, second time the summary reflects the commitment back and earns "that's right," third time a specific implementation question forces her to detail how the commitment will actually happen.

Any inconsistency across the three is intelligence — something is not aligned. Not an accusation. Information. Find the gap. Name it.

Underneath is the definite verification — the non-drifter does not accept a commitment at face value. The non-drifter verifies with definiteness. A definite verification catches the drift between what someone says and what someone intends. The drifter hears "yes" and moves on. The non-drifter hears "yes" and runs two more passes before believing it.

## Pronoun Analysis

Two layers. Not interchangeable.

**Layer 1 — Deception context.**
People being deceptive use more words, more third-person pronouns, more complex sentences. Heavy "we/they/them" = distancing from the deception. The inflation tell: the number of words grows with the lie. When she is over-explaining, adding unnecessary complexity, shifting to third-person constructions — the deception is producing the words. The more words it takes to say something simple, the less likely the simple version is true.

**Layer 2 — Power context.**
Heavy "I/me/my" = less important in the decision chain. The person who speaks constantly in first person is nervous about committing — they are managing their exposure. Heavy "we/they/them" in a power context = either distancing from a deception, or the actual decision maker keeping options open. The harder it is to get a first-person pronoun out of someone's mouth, the more authority they actually hold.

## Hidden Cards

Every conversation contains at least three pieces of information that, if uncovered, would change everything. The information is there. It is not being offered. The work is creating the conditions where it surfaces.

Known knowns: what both parties know both know. Known unknowns: what one party knows they do not know. Unknown unknowns: what neither party knows they do not know. The hidden cards live in the third category.

**Unguarded moments.** The most honest signals arrive at the edges: before the conversation settles, when something catches her off guard, the last seconds before goodbye. The mask is not fully on at those moments.

**The three explanations for behavior that seems irrational.**
She is not crazy. She is never crazy.
1. Ill-informed: she is working from bad information.
2. Constrained: something she cannot or will not say is limiting what she can do.
3. Other interests: goals you cannot see are actually driving her behavior.

"She's just crazy" ends inquiry. And it is almost never accurate.

## The Box Response

When she frames the interaction as a category rather than an exchange: "You're just like every other guy who comes up to me." "Is this your move?" "Let me guess — you're going to ask for my number." "Men always do this."

The instinct is to defend: "No, I'm not like that, I'm actually..." Defense confirms the frame. The moment you explain why you are different from the category, you have accepted the category as the frame and are now arguing from inside it. The category wins. She put you in a box — defending means you are now inside the box arguing your way out.

The response is to label the feeling that built the box. Not to defend, not to explain, not to charm past it. Label the feeling underneath: "It seems like someone did this before and it didn't feel good." "It sounds like you've had this conversation enough times that it stopped being flattering a while ago."

The box dissolves when the specific feeling underneath it is named. She is not actually accusing you of being like every other guy. She is protecting herself from the feeling that the last guy who approached her produced. The accusation is the armor. The feeling underneath is the person. When the label hits the feeling, the armor becomes unnecessary.

Underneath is the definite label — the non-drifter does not drift into defense when accused. The non-drifter labels with definiteness: names the specific feeling underneath the accusation, holds steady, lets the label work.

**The fairness flag:** When she says anything framing the situation as a question of fairness — "I just don't think that's fair," "I want to be fair to you," "to be fair..." — this is not a statement about fairness. It is a power signal. She is establishing terms. The response is not to agree with the terms. The response is to label what is actually happening: "It seems like there's something specific you want me to understand." Fairness language invites investigation, not agreement.

## The Invisible Audience

Every conversation has an audience that is not present. She is not making decisions alone — she is making decisions that she will immediately report to someone else. Her best friend. Her roommate. Her sister. The group chat. The person she is going to text about this interaction as soon as it ends.

The invisible audience changes what she can agree to in the moment. She might want to give you her number but know that her friend will judge the story of how she gave it. She might want to stay and talk but know that her roommate is waiting and will ask what took so long. She might be into it but unable to act because someone in her life has a veto she cannot override in real time.

The diagnostic question: "Who is she going to tell about this conversation?" When a conversation felt warm but ended abruptly, or she seemed interested but then pulled back, or she agreed to something and then did not follow through — the invisible audience is a primary explanation. The question surfaces the constraint she was operating under that had nothing to do with you.

In Pre-Approach Prep: consider the invisible audience when calibrating. Is she with friends? Is she alone but clearly waiting for someone? The invisible audience shapes how much freedom she has to engage openly. When she is with friends, the approach is being evaluated by the group — not just by her. When she is alone, the invisible audience is still present: the person she will tell about this later is still shaping what she feels she can do.

## Emotional Regulation

The best-designed strategy in the world is only as good as the emotional regulation of the person executing it.

The hostage mentality mechanism: fight-or-flight from the reptilian brain, or the flood of emotion from the limbic system, overwhelms the rational processing in the neocortex. When you are flooded — activated, angry, hurt, desperate — the neocortex is offline and none of the tools are available.

**Anger should not be suppressed.** Suppressing a negative emotion gives it more underground power. It should also not be acted out. It should be brought to the surface — named, acknowledged, released. This is what the six-tool arsenal does with her emotions, and it is what works with your own emotions when you arrive flooded. Label the emotion first. Let it exhaust itself. Then the conversation can proceed.

**The beat — the pause protocol.** Pause before responding. One breath between hearing and speaking. That is the entire technique. The gap between hearing something that activates you and responding to it is where the emotional charge dissipates and the clear response surfaces. Without the gap, the emotion speaks. With it, you speak. The beat is not hesitation — it is the deliberate creation of space between the stimulus and the response. When you feel the surge — anger, defensiveness, the impulse to react — take the beat. The surge is the limbic system firing before the rational brain has caught up. The pause gives the rational brain time to arrive.

Underneath is the definite pause — the non-drifter does not react. The non-drifter pauses with definiteness — not out of paralysis (that is the indecision trap) but out of the deliberate choice to respond rather than react.

**Emotional regulation applies to any high-stakes interaction.** When you arrive flooded — activated about a situation, a rejection, a conversation that went wrong — the six tools do not help until the swamp is drained. Effective pause, minimal encouragers, hold space. Get back to the neocortex before diagnosis or tool assignment. Trying to operate from a flooded limbic system produces nothing. The work cannot start until you can hear it.
